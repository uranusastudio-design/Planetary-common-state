[1] Introduction
[2] 1.1 Background
[3] The long-term stability of complex systems has become one of the central scientific challenges across modern physics, Earth system science, ecology, engineering, and network theory. Although these disciplines investigate different physical processes, they share a common objective: understanding how large-scale coupled systems maintain stability under continuous perturbations.
[4] In recent decades, substantial progress has been achieved in describing individual components of the Earth system. Thermodynamics characterizes energy transport and entropy production. Fluid dynamics explains atmospheric and oceanic circulation. Geophysics models lithospheric and gravitational processes. Information theory quantifies communication and biological complexity, while systems ecology investigates resilience and regime shifts.
[5] Despite these advances, these quantities remain largely confined within disciplinary boundaries. Existing models typically quantify isolated subsystems, whereas the degradation of real-world systems emerges through the continuous interaction among multiple physical processes.
[6] Consequently, no generally accepted macroscopic representation currently exists that describes the collective degradation state of an integrated Earth–civilization system.
[8] 1.2 Motivation
[9] The absence of a unified representation is not caused by a lack of physical observables.
[10] Instead, it arises because existing observables were developed independently for different scientific purposes.
[11] For example,
[12] radiative imbalance measures thermal disequilibrium,
[13] atmospheric circulation indices describe dynamical instability,
[14] biodiversity indices characterize ecological degradation,
[15] infrastructure indicators measure structural resilience,
[16] information metrics quantify communication efficiency.
[17] Each observable captures one aspect of system evolution.
[18] However, none is intended to represent the overall state of a coupled planetary system.
[19] As a result, modern science possesses numerous accurate measurements, yet lacks a common mathematical language for describing how these heterogeneous processes collectively constrain macroscopic system evolution.
[20] This fragmentation becomes particularly important near critical transitions, where failures emerging in different physical domains interact nonlinearly rather than independently.
[22] 1.3 Scope of This Work
[23] The objective of the present work is not to introduce a new fundamental law of physics.
[24] Nor do we propose modifications to General Relativity, Quantum Mechanics, Classical Thermodynamics, or Statistical Mechanics.
[25] Instead, we introduce an effective macroscopic framework for representing coupled constraints acting across multiple interacting subsystems.
[26] The proposed framework is intended for complex systems whose behavior cannot be adequately characterized using any single physical observable.
[27] Typical examples include Earth systems, ecological systems, infrastructure networks, and large-scale socio-technical systems.
[28] Accordingly, the present theory should be interpreted as an effective representation at macroscopic scales rather than as a microscopic fundamental theory.
[30] 1.4 Central Hypothesis
[31] The central hypothesis of this work is that the apparent fragmentation of physical observables reflects an underlying fragmentation of system constraints rather than of physical laws themselves.
[32] To formalize this idea, we introduce a Unified Constraint Operator,
[33] L\boxed{ \mathbb{L} }L​
[34] which represents the coupled constraints governing the evolution of macroscopic systems.
[35] Unlike conventional state variables,
[36] L\mathbb{L}L
[37] is not itself a measurable physical quantity.
[38] Instead, it is an effective operator whose observable projections correspond to measurable physical domains such as thermal imbalance, structural degradation, information loss, flow instability, energetic efficiency, and other coupled processes.
[39] The measurable quantities are therefore interpreted as projections of a common operator rather than as isolated variables.
[41] 1.5 Main Contributions
[42] The principal contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
[43] We formulate a unified mathematical representation for heterogeneous constraint processes in coupled macroscopic systems.
[44] We introduce the concept of a Unified Constraint Operator whose observable components correspond to measurable physical domains.
[45] We construct a constraint geometry in which macroscopic evolution is represented as trajectories on a multidimensional manifold.
[46] We demonstrate how existing observables from Earth system science may be interpreted within this unified framework without modifying established physical theories.
[47] We establish a mathematical foundation that permits future empirical calibration using observational data rather than introducing additional phenomenological constants.

[157] 3. A Unified Residual Representation for Coupled Earth–Civilization Systems
[158] 3.1 Motivation
[159] Modern physics has achieved remarkable success in describing individual physical processes. Heat transfer, fluid dynamics, gravitation, chemical reactions, information theory, and complex networks each possess mature mathematical descriptions. Nevertheless, these quantities are almost always treated as independent observables belonging to different scientific disciplines.
[160] For large-scale coupled systems such as the Earth system, this disciplinary separation becomes a limitation rather than an advantage. Irreversible degradation rarely occurs within a single physical process. Instead, it emerges through the interaction of multiple residual processes acting simultaneously across thermal, material, informational, structural, energetic, and societal domains.
[161] Consequently, there is currently no macroscopic state representation capable of describing the collective residual state of an integrated Earth–civilization system.
[162] The objective of this work is therefore not to replace existing physical variables, but to introduce a common mathematical representation that allows these fragmented residual quantities to be described within a unified framework.

[621] Chapter 11
[622] Discussion
[624] 11.1 Scientific Position
[625] The Unified Constraint Theory (UCT) should not be interpreted as a replacement for established physical theories.
[626] Instead, it provides an effective macroscopic representation describing how heterogeneous physical constraints jointly influence the evolution of coupled systems.
[627] Accordingly, UCT complements rather than competes with thermodynamics, fluid mechanics, statistical mechanics, information theory, and Earth system science.
[629] 11.2 Relation to Existing Theories
[630] The proposed framework shares conceptual similarities with several existing approaches while remaining fundamentally distinct.
[631] Thermodynamics describes irreversible energy dissipation.
[632] Statistical mechanics characterizes collective behavior emerging from microscopic dynamics.
[633] Earth system science investigates interactions among planetary subsystems.
[634] Complex systems theory studies nonlinear feedbacks and emergent behavior.
[635] UCT differs from these approaches by introducing a unified mathematical representation for coupled constraints rather than focusing on individual physical processes.
[637] 11.3 Interpretation of the Constraint Operator
[638] The Unified Constraint Operator
[639] L\mathbb LL
[640] is not intended to represent a new physical field.
[641] Neither is it a conserved quantity or a new fundamental interaction.
[642] Instead,
[643] L\mathbb LL
[644] is an effective mathematical operator whose observable projections are constructed from measurable physical variables.
[645] Therefore,
[646] the validity of UCT depends entirely on empirical predictive performance.
[648] 11.4 Scope of Applicability
[649] The present framework is designed for macroscopic coupled systems,
[650] including
[651] Earth systems,
[652] ecological systems,
[653] infrastructure networks,
[654] socio-technical systems,
[655] coupled environmental processes.
[656] The framework is not intended to replace microscopic theories such as Quantum Mechanics,
[657] nor is it intended to modify General Relativity.
[659] 11.5 Limitations
[660] Several important limitations should be emphasized.
[661] First,
[662] the present work introduces the theoretical framework only.
[663] The projection operators,
[664] coupling coefficients,
[665] and assimilation procedures require empirical calibration using observational datasets.
[666] Second,
[667] the proposed constraint geometry is an effective representation.
[668] Alternative geometric formulations may exist.
[669] Third,
[670] the present study does not claim that the chosen projection space is unique.
[671] Different applications may require different projection operators.
[673] 11.6 Future Work
[674] Future work should include
[675] global satellite calibration,
[676] machine-learning estimation of projection operators,
[677] Bayesian uncertainty quantification,
[678] regional Earth-system applications,
[679] early-warning prediction of critical transitions,
[680] real-time global monitoring.
